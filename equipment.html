Central Plant

The basement makes the building habitable.

Heat is added or rejected, water is moved, electricity becomes useful work. Five machines do most of it.

Centrifugal
Cooling · 200–6,000 tons

Centrifugal Chiller

A high-speed impeller compresses refrigerant vapor. Heat absorbed in the evaporator (chilled-water side) is rejected through the condenser (condenser-water side). The workhorse of large commercial cooling — best at part-load when staged correctly.

Screw Chiller

Two intermeshing helical rotors compress vapor between their lobes and the casing. More tolerant of liquid carry-over than centrifugal, with strong part-load performance. Common mid-size choice for offices, schools, and hospitals.

Hydronics

The water side, in one diagram.

Five loops, four heat exchanges, two state changes. Every commercial chilled-water plant lives inside this picture.

Refrigeration Cycle

The four-stroke engine that cools your building.

Every chiller, heat pump, and DX rooftop unit is some variation of this loop. Four state points, two pressures.

The four states

① Suction
Low-pressure vapor, slightly superheated, leaving the evaporator. The compressor pulls from here.
② Discharge
High-pressure, high-temperature vapor leaving the compressor. The hottest point in the cycle.
③ Liquid
High-pressure, sub-cooled liquid leaving the condenser. All the rejected heat is now out of the loop.
④ Flash
Low-pressure two-phase mixture, after the expansion device. Very cold. The boiler-room of the cycle.

Plotted on a pressure-enthalpy diagram, these four points form a near-rectangle. Compressor efficiency, evaporator approach, condenser approach, and refrigerant choice each move one of those corners — and each of those moves shows up directly in kW/ton.

Controls

The brains behind the iron.

Three tiers — field, supervisory, enterprise. Each one solves a problem the tier below it can't.

Field controllers (DDC)

The microcontrollers that drive valves, dampers, and VFDs. One per VAV, one per AHU, multiple per chiller. Run the BACnet/MS-TP bus.

Supervisory / JACE

The "head end." Aggregates the field bus, hosts global logic, exposes BACnet/IP, runs schedules, and pushes data to the historian.

Edge gateway

Pulls from supervisory controllers and pushes to cloud — typically over MQTT or HTTPS. Where data tagging (Haystack, Brick) usually lives.

Cloud historian + UI

Time-series database, analytics, dashboards, the mobile app, and the integrations with CMMS / IWMS / utility platforms.

Reference

A working glossary.

The acronyms an operator hears on a Tuesday morning, defined in one line.

AHU
Air-Handling Unit — central conditioning and air-moving box.
BACnet
Open building-automation protocol; the lingua franca of HVAC controls since 1995.
BMS / BAS
Building Management System / Building Automation System — same thing, different decades.
BTU
British Thermal Unit; 1 BTU raises 1 lb water by 1°F. 12,000 BTU/h = 1 ton of cooling.
CFM
Cubic feet per minute — standard unit of airflow.
CHWS / CHWR
Chilled-water supply / return.
COP
Coefficient of Performance — useful heat out divided by electrical work in.
CWS / CWR
Condenser-water supply / return (chiller heat-rejection loop).
DAT
Discharge-air temperature (from an AHU).
DCV
Demand-controlled ventilation; ties outdoor-air rate to measured CO₂.
DDC
Direct Digital Control — microprocessor-based controls (i.e. since 1985).
DX
Direct expansion — refrigerant evaporates directly inside an air-side coil.
ECM
Electronically Commutated Motor — efficient fan motor, default in modern equipment.
ERV
Energy Recovery Ventilator — transfers heat (and moisture) between exhaust and OA.
FDD
Fault Detection & Diagnostics — rule-based or ML pattern matching on live BMS data.
FCU
Fan Coil Unit — small terminal AHU, one or two rooms.
HBMS
Holistic Building Management System — the data and intelligence layer above a BMS.
HEPA
High-Efficiency Particulate Air — ≥99.97% on 0.3 µm particles.
HWS / HWR
Hot-water supply / return.
IPLV
Integrated Part-Load Value — weighted-average efficiency over typical load mix.
JACE
Java Application Control Engine — Tridium's supervisory controller (genericized).
kW/ton
Chiller efficiency — input electric kW per 12,000 BTU/h of cooling.
LonWorks
Legacy open protocol (Echelon); still common in elevators and lighting.
MAU
Makeup Air Unit — 100% outdoor-air handler.
MERV
Minimum Efficiency Reporting Value — filter rating, 1–16.
Modbus
Simple register-based protocol; ubiquitous on chillers, meters, drives.
MQTT
Lightweight publish/subscribe protocol; common for cloud telemetry.
NDIR
Non-Dispersive Infrared — the dominant CO₂ sensor technology.
OA / RA / EA
Outdoor / return / exhaust air.
OAT / OAH
Outdoor-air temperature / humidity.
PM2.5
Particulate matter ≤2.5 µm; the IAQ metric most tied to health outcomes.
PID
Proportional-Integral-Derivative — the workhorse control loop.
RH
Relative humidity — water vapor present vs. saturation at that temperature.
RTU
Rooftop Unit — packaged AHU + compressor, dropped on the roof.
SAT
Supply-air temperature.
SEER
Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio — DX system efficiency.
VAV
Variable Air Volume — terminal box that modulates airflow.
VFD
Variable Frequency Drive — varies motor speed; the dominant energy-saving device.
WBT
Wet-bulb temperature — the cooling-tower's theoretical limit.
ΔP / ΔT
Differential pressure / temperature — across a coil, filter, pump, or loop.
