The mechanical room

Every system we touch, explained.

Chillers, boilers, air-handling units, terminal units, pumps, towers, and the controls that orchestrate them.

Air-Handling Unit

Where outside air becomes "the air in the room."

An AHU is the central organ of an HVAC system. Outdoor air is mixed with return air, filtered, conditioned by cooling and heating coils, moved by a fan, and pushed to the zones it serves. Modern AHUs run on variable speed, modulate dampers and coil valves continuously, and report a dozen or more points back to the BMS.

Outdoor & return air mixing

Chilled-Water Plant

The cold heart of a commercial building.

A chiller removes heat from water. That cold water, typically 44 °F, is then pumped through the cooling coils of every air-handling unit and fan-coil in the building. The heat picked up by the water has to go somewhere, so a second loop (condenser water) carries it up to the cooling tower, which rejects it to the sky by evaporation.

VAV Terminal Unit

The volume knob for every zone.

A variable-air-volume box sits between the AHU's main supply duct and the zones it serves. It modulates a damper to vary the airflow into the zone, and, optionally, runs a small reheat coil for cold-weather perimeter zones. Each VAV reports its zone temperature, damper position, flow, and (in good designs) CO₂ back to the BMS.

The science

HVAC is just psychrometrics, applied at scale.

Every decision an HVAC system makes, every degree of cooling, every drop of condensate, every BTU of reheat, is a path across this chart. Mastering it is the difference between a building that hits its setpoints and a building that hits its setpoints efficiently.

Dry-bulb temperature on the horizontal axis. Humidity ratio on the vertical. Curves of constant relative humidity sweep through the middle. The comfort zone, the rectangle most occupants will tolerate without complaint, is a small island in the middle.

A short history of HVAC controls

How the building got smart.

Five generations, each one solving the previous generation's bottleneck.

1902
First mechanical AC

Willis Carrier installs the first system in a Brooklyn printing plant, to control humidity for ink. Comfort cooling comes later.

1950s · Pneumatic era
Compressed air controls

Buildings run on a fragile, beautifully analog network of compressed air signals. Operators carry a screwdriver and institutional memory.

1985 · DDC arrives
Direct Digital Control

Microcontrollers replace pneumatic logic. Setpoints can be changed from a workstation, but each vendor speaks a private dialect.

2000 · Open protocols
BACnet, LonWorks, Modbus

The lid comes off. Multi-vendor systems become specifiable. Historical data, however, lives and dies on the operator workstation.

2015 · IP & IoT
Cloud historians, edge gateways

Storing every point at one-minute resolution becomes economical. The new bottleneck shifts from capturing data to organizing it.

2025 · HBMS & digital twins
Holistic Building Management

Tagged data models (Haystack, Brick) finally let owners query across vendors and across buildings. The building, at last, knows itself.
