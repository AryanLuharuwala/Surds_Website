Psychrometrics for HVAC Operators

The chart every HVAC operator has seen on a poster, but few have actually used to make a decision. A practical walkthrough — with examples from buildings we run.

by Alyson Park, P.E. · Founding Principal
11 min read · May 2026
A psychrometric chart with a comfort zone overlaid. Every HVAC decision moves a point on this chart.

Every commercial HVAC training program puts a psychrometric chart on the wall and walks past it. Operators are told it exists, told it's important, and given a quiz where they identify dry-bulb temperature on the bottom axis. Then they're handed a wrench and never asked to look at it again.

That's a shame. The psychrometric chart is the single best tool an HVAC operator has for thinking about what their building is actually doing. Used well, it tells you why your cooling coil is producing wet air, why your reheat is fighting your cooling, why your economizer is dumping outdoor air at exactly the wrong moment, and why your dehumidifier can't keep up.

This article is the introduction nobody gave you. We'll walk through what the chart shows, how to read a state point, what the four basic HVAC processes look like as lines on the chart, and three examples from real buildings.

What the chart shows

The psychrometric chart plots the properties of moist air — specifically, dry air mixed with some amount of water vapor — under standard atmospheric pressure. Every point on the chart represents one specific condition of air. Knowing the state point tells you everything thermodynamic you need to know about that air sample.

Five quantities matter, and they're not independent — pick any two and the rest are determined.

  1. Dry-bulb temperature (DBT) — what a regular thermometer reads. The horizontal axis.
  2. Humidity ratio (W) — pounds of water vapor per pound of dry air. The vertical axis.
  3. Relative humidity (RH) — water vapor present compared to saturation at that temperature, as a percentage. Curved lines sweeping up through the chart.
  4. Wet-bulb temperature (WBT) — what a thermometer wrapped in a wet wick reads. Lines sloping down-and-right.
  5. Enthalpy (h) — total energy per pound of dry air. Lines nearly parallel to wet-bulb lines, in BTU per pound dry air.

The four basic processes

Almost every HVAC process is one of four moves across the chart. Read them like the moves of a chess piece.

1. Sensible heating

Pure sensible heating — through a hot-water coil, an electric heater, or a heat-pump condenser — is a horizontal line moving to the right. Dry-bulb temperature increases; humidity ratio is constant. Relative humidity falls as a side-effect, because the same amount of water vapor in warmer air is a smaller fraction of saturation.

2. Sensible cooling

The mirror image. A horizontal line moving left. This is what happens when an air sample brushes past a cooling coil whose surface temperature is above the air's dew point — no condensation, just temperature drop. Relative humidity rises.

3. Cooling and dehumidification

The most common path. A cooling coil at a surface temperature below the entering dew point. Water condenses out of the air. The process line moves down-and-to-the-left, toward the saturation curve. By the time the air leaves the coil, it's typically at or very near saturation, at some temperature in the low- to mid-fifties.

4. Humidification

Adding moisture. Two flavors. Isothermal humidification (steam) is a near-vertical line — moisture up, temperature roughly constant. Adiabatic humidification (evaporative — wetted media or ultrasonic spray) is a line along a constant wet-bulb temperature — moisture up, dry-bulb down. The second is the basis of evaporative cooling, and of cooling towers.

The comfort zone

ASHRAE Standard 55 defines a region of the chart that 80% of typical occupants will find acceptable for sedentary indoor work. In practice it's a small rectangle: roughly 68 to 78 °F dry-bulb, and 30% to 60% RH. The exact boundaries depend on clothing, activity, and air velocity, but the rectangle is a reasonable approximation for office and institutional buildings.

Note what's outside the comfort zone: every condition in which somebody is uncomfortable. Hot and dry. Cold and damp. Warm and humid. Each unhappy zone has a name and a fix.

Three examples from real buildings

Example 1: A "cold-and-clammy" complaint

A tenant called: "the conference room is cold but the air feels heavy." When we pulled the data, the zone was 69 °F at 64% RH. Plotted on the chart, that's at the edge of the comfort zone — technically in spec on temperature, well outside on humidity. What we found: the AHU was cooling with a low supply-air temperature setpoint (52 °F) and then dialing the airflow way down to avoid overcooling. Result: small volumes of very cold, very saturated air that condensed back out moisture in the duct and never properly mixed in the room. The fix was to raise the supply-air setpoint to 57 °F, increase airflow, and let the room temperature regulate by volume rather than by temperature. The "heavy" feel disappeared.

Example 2: The economizer that wasn't

In a downtown office, the AHU's economizer was supposed to use outdoor air when it was cool enough — the classic "free cooling." The control sequence used dry-bulb temperature: "open economizer when OA < 65 °F." On a humid May morning, the building was bringing in 60 °F outdoor air at 95% RH — which, by enthalpy, was carrying about as much energy as 78 °F dry indoor air. The chiller had to work twice as hard to dehumidify it. The fix is to switch to enthalpy-based control: "open economizer when h(OA) < h(RA)." The chart makes this obvious; the dry-bulb-only sequence does not.

Example 3: The museum gallery

The Linden Avenue Museum's textile gallery requires 50% RH ± 3%. In April, the outdoor air is often around 45 °F and 70% RH — at a humidity ratio well below the gallery's target. Bringing in ventilation air and heating it dry-bulb (process 1, sensible heating) drops the relative humidity to about 20%. The gallery needs both heat and moisture addition. The chart shows you can't get there with a heating coil alone — you need steam humidification, sized to the OA flow at that outdoor condition. The chart is also what the original designer used to size the humidifier; using it to verify what's installed is how we catch that the humidifier is undersized for the design day.

Things the chart doesn't show, but every operator should know

The chart is for standard atmospheric pressure. At altitude, all the values shift. Denver requires a different chart from Chicago. Most controls software handles this correctly; some don't. Worth verifying once.

The chart assumes the air is well-mixed. Stratification in a poorly-mixed room, or short-circuiting at a diffuser, means the air the occupant feels is not the air your return-duct sensor reports. The chart doesn't catch this; field measurement does.

Latent and sensible loads vary independently. A conference room with twelve people has a high latent load (breath, perspiration) but a modest sensible load. A south-facing office with no people has the reverse. A control sequence tuned for one is wrong for the other. The chart helps you see which kind of load you have.

Get it on the wall — and look at it

The chart isn't a quiz item. It's an analytical tool. The next time you have a comfort complaint, a humidity excursion, or a "why is the AHU doing that" question, draw the state points on the chart before you do anything else. The answer is often visible in ten seconds.

The single best upgrade we recommend for a facility operations office is a large, framed, laminated psychrometric chart on the wall, with a dry-erase marker next to it. The cheapest, most useful tool in the room.
