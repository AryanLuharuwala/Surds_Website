THE CHILLED-WATER
PLANT, TOP TO BOTTOM.

Chillers, towers, pumps and one stubborn number — ΔT. How the cold gets made and moved.

Most large buildings make cold in one place and spend it everywhere else. The chilled-water plant is that one place: a few big machines and a lot of pumps, tied together by water that leaves cold and comes back warm.

Make the cold

The chiller is the engine. It pulls heat out of the chilled-water loop and dumps it into the condenser-water loop. Efficiency lives in two resets: lift the chilled-water supply temperature when you can, and drop the condenser-water temperature when the towers allow.

Reject the heat

Cooling towers throw that condenser heat to the sky by evaporating a little water. Variable-speed fans chase a setpoint; the closer the tower gets to wet-bulb, the less work the chiller does downstream.

Low ΔT syndrome is the quiet tax on half the plants we walk into.

Move it well

Pumps push the water around. The number that tells you whether the loop is healthy is ΔT — the gap between supply and return. Design it for 12 °F, run it at 6, and you're pumping twice the water for the same cooling. Recover ΔT and pumping energy falls off a cliff.
