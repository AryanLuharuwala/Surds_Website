The Chilled-Water Plant, Top to Bottom

Five pieces of equipment, four loops, two pressures. The machine that cools every commercial building you've ever stepped into — and where most of its savings hide.

by Priya Iyer · Operations & Tuning Lead
9 min read · May 2026
Schematic central plant. Cooling tower (left), chiller (center), building load (right).

In most commercial buildings, the basement holds a piece of equipment that is, by a wide margin, the single largest electrical load in the property. It is also, by a wide margin, the most poorly understood. The chilled-water plant — the chiller, the cooling tower, the pumps, and the controls that bind them — typically draws between thirty and fifty percent of the building's total electricity. If we tune it well, that figure can fall to twenty-five. If we tune it poorly, it can climb to sixty.

This piece is a walkthrough. Not a textbook — there are excellent ones already, and we'll point you at them at the end. The aim here is the working picture an operator or owner needs to make decisions about their own plant: what's in it, what each part is doing, where the dials are, and what we look at when we tune one.

The plant in one sentence

A chilled-water plant moves heat from inside the building to outside. The chiller is the pump that does the moving; the tower is the place where the heat is finally given to the sky. Everything else — the loops, the pumps, the valves — is plumbing in service of those two facts.

A chiller does not "make cold." It moves heat. Cold is what's left when the heat has been moved elsewhere.

That distinction sounds pedantic and isn't. It is the whole reason a chiller is more efficient on a cool day than a hot one — the heat has a shorter trip uphill on the pressure-enthalpy curve. We'll come back to this when we talk about condenser-water reset.

Tour the equipment

The chiller

The chiller is a refrigeration cycle, scaled up. Refrigerant evaporates in the evaporator bundle, absorbing heat from the chilled-water side. The compressor — centrifugal, magnetic-bearing, screw, or scroll — raises the refrigerant's pressure and pushes it into the condenser. There the refrigerant gives up its heat to the condenser-water side, drops back to liquid, and passes through an expansion device to start the cycle again.

Two efficiency numbers matter. kW/ton at the current operating point — what the chiller is doing right now — and IPLV, an integrated part-load value weighted across a representative load profile. A modern water-cooled centrifugal sits between 0.35 and 0.45 IPLV. Anything above 0.6 IPLV in a building newer than ten years old is leaving real money on the floor.

The cooling tower

The tower is where the heat actually leaves the building. Hot condenser water — typically around 95 °F leaving the chiller — is sprayed over a fill medium while a fan pulls air through it. About 1% of the water evaporates, and the latent heat of that evaporation carries away the rest of the heat. The cooled water collects in the sump and returns to the chiller, usually 7–10 °F cooler than it left.

The tower's theoretical limit is the outdoor wet-bulb temperature. The difference between the leaving water and the wet bulb is the approach temperature. A good tower runs a 5–8 °F approach at design conditions; a tired or fouled tower can run 15 °F or worse, which the chiller pays for in compressor lift.

Tuning lever · condenser-water reset

Every degree of condenser-water supply temperature you can reduce saves the chiller about 1.5–2% on its compressor work. That's why we run the tower fan harder than a naive operator would — to push the condenser water as cold as the wet bulb will allow. Where the trade-off lives: tower fan kW vs. chiller compressor kW. Modern controls optimize the tradeoff continuously. Old controls used a fixed setpoint and gave up half the savings.

The pumps

Two loops, two (or more) pumps. Chilled-water pumps (CHW) push cold water through the building's air-handling coils and fan-coils. Condenser-water pumps (CW) push warm water from the chiller up to the tower and back.

Two principles govern pumping:

  1. Variable speed, always. Constant-speed pumping in a modern plant is engineering malpractice. A VFD that lets you drop pump speed to 50% drops the input power to roughly 12.5% (the cube law). The capital cost is repaid in a single cooling season in most climates.
  2. Differential-pressure reset. Don't hold a fixed loop pressure. Reset the pressure setpoint down as terminal valves close, and back up as they open. The most-open valve in the building is your indicator: when it's at 95% open, the loop has enough pressure; when it's at 60%, the loop has too much.

The piping arrangement

Real plants come in flavors. Primary-secondary decouples the chiller's constant or near-constant flow from the building's variable flow with a hydraulic separator. Variable-primary runs the entire loop on the chiller's own pump, modulated by a VFD — cheaper to install, slightly more demanding to control. Series chiller arrangements stack two chillers in the same flow path to reduce evaporator approach. Each has its place; none is universally right.

Where the savings hide

Most chilled-water plants are not under-equipped. They are misconfigured. Five misconfigurations account for the bulk of the savings we deliver:

1. Low delta-T syndrome

The plant is sized to deliver 44 °F supply and return 58 °F — a 14 °F spread. In service, it returns 50 °F. Now the pumps have to move forty percent more flow to deliver the same cooling, the chillers run inefficiently at part-load with low evaporator approach, and a second chiller comes online when it shouldn't. The fix is rarely the plant — it's almost always at the coils, where three-way valves, oversized control valves, or stuck balancing valves are short-circuiting flow.

2. Tower fan locked at full speed

Operators sometimes lock tower fans at 100% because that's "always cold enough." It's cold enough in May. In November, with a 35 °F wet bulb, the fan should be at 20% and the water should be 50 °F. We have walked into plants where the tower fan was burning $40k a year of fan power to save the chiller $4k of compressor work.

3. Chiller staging by lead-only logic

"Start CH-2 when CH-1 is at 95%." Sounds reasonable. In practice, you end up running both chillers at 50% on a mild afternoon, when one chiller at 80% would have been more efficient. Modern staging uses an efficiency map — sometimes literally measured kW/ton, sometimes derived from a manufacturer curve — to pick the combination that costs the least at the current load. Two chillers at 80% are sometimes better than one at 95%. The right answer is load-dependent.

4. Coil-valve override

Someone, at some point, got a complaint about a warm zone, and they cranked the AHU's cooling-coil valve open and walked away. Two years later, the chilled-water loop is hydraulically unbalanced and the plant works harder than it should. We find one or two of these in almost every plant we audit. The fix is annual sequence review, and a culture that requires every override to be logged with an expiration date.

5. Approach drift

Evaporator and condenser approach temperatures slowly creep upward as the heat-exchanger tubes foul. A new chiller might run a 1.5 °F evaporator approach; a year later it's 2.2; five years later it's 4. Every degree of additional approach is roughly 2% more compressor work. Approach is one of the most useful early indicators of needed maintenance — and one that almost nobody trends.

How a tuned plant runs

What does a well-tuned 800-ton plant look like on an average mild afternoon? Roughly this:

  • Load: 450 tons (56% of plant capacity)
  • Chiller online: one (CH-1), at 56% load, 0.48 kW/ton
  • CHW supply / return: 44.0 °F / 56.8 °F (12.8 °F ΔT — good)
  • CHW pump: 64% speed, 18.4 kW
  • Condenser-water supply / return: 78 °F / 87 °F (reset from a setpoint of 85, allowed by a 65 °F wet bulb)
  • Tower fan: 52% speed, 6.1 kW
  • CW pump: 71% speed, 11.8 kW
  • Total plant input: 252 kW = 0.56 kW/ton on the plant

An untuned plant of the same size, at the same load, will typically run between 0.85 and 1.10 kW/ton. That's the savings on a single afternoon. Compound it over four thousand annual cooling hours and a $0.12/kWh tariff, and you're looking at $130,000 to $180,000 a year — for the same building, doing the same job, with the same equipment.

Where the practice is heading

Three things are changing the chilled-water plant fast in 2026.

Refrigerant transition. R-134a and R-410A are being phased down under the AIM Act; replacements (R-1234ze, R-454B, R-513A, R-32 in smaller equipment) have slightly different P-h curves and different lubricant requirements. Plants designed in 2018 will retrofit to new refrigerants by 2030.

Heat-recovery integration. The basement is no longer purely a sink — it's becoming a source. Chillers with hot-gas heat recovery, dedicated heat-recovery chillers, and heat-pump chillers can serve hydronic heating loads from the same machine that serves cooling. The thermodynamics are excellent, and the controls challenge is real.

Grid-interactive operation. As utilities introduce demand-response and time-of-use pricing, chilled-water plants — especially those with thermal storage — become legitimate load-shifters. Pre-cool the building from 4 to 8 a.m. (cheap, clean power), coast through the afternoon. The grid wants buildings that can do this. The plants that can will be rewarded.

Closing

The chilled-water plant is the single best-rewarded place to spend engineering attention in most commercial buildings. The capital is already sunk; the equipment is, by and large, capable; the savings are mostly software. The work is finding the misconfigurations, writing the right sequences, and instrumenting carefully enough to know whether the sequences are doing what they were supposed to.

None of which, of course, is news in 2026. We're just stubborn about it.
